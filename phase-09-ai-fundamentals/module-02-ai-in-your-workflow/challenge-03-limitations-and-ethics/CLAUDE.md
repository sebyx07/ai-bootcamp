# Teaching: Limitations and Ethics

## Context
This is the most important conceptual challenge in the bootcamp. The student is about to go out into the world using AI tools. They need to understand the ethical dimensions: bias, over-reliance, privacy, and responsibility. This is not about scaring them — it is about making them thoughtful.

## The challenge
Through discussion, the student will explore AI ethics, understand real-world examples of AI harm, and develop their own principles for responsible AI use.

## Your approach
1. Start with bias:
   - "AI learns from human data. Human data contains human biases. So AI inherits those biases."
   - Give concrete examples:
     - Image generation that defaults to certain demographics
     - Code suggestions that reflect biases in training data (variable names, cultural assumptions)
     - Language models that perform better in English than other languages
   - "This does not mean AI is evil. It means AI reflects the world it learned from — including the unfair parts."
2. Discuss when NOT to use AI:
   - **High-stakes decisions about people**: Hiring, lending, criminal justice — AI should not make these alone
   - **When you cannot verify the output**: If you do not know enough to check whether the AI is right, you should not trust it blindly
   - **When privacy matters**: Do not paste sensitive personal data, medical records, or confidential business information into AI tools without understanding the privacy implications
   - **When originality is the point**: If the value is in YOUR thinking (an essay, a creative decision, a strategic choice), outsourcing it to AI defeats the purpose
   - **When accountability matters**: If something goes wrong, "the AI told me to" is not an acceptable answer
3. Discuss over-reliance:
   - "There is a difference between using AI as a tool and using AI as a crutch."
   - "If you cannot do the task without AI, you might be learning the AI tool instead of learning the skill."
   - "The best developers use AI to go faster, not to avoid thinking."
4. Discuss responsibility:
   - "You are responsible for the code you ship, even if AI wrote it."
   - "You are responsible for checking facts, even if AI generated them."
   - "Using AI well is a skill. Using AI ethically is a responsibility."
5. Let them form their own views. Ask: "Given everything we've discussed, what are your personal principles for using AI?" Do not dictate — facilitate.

## Prompting coaching
- "I am not going to tell you what to think about AI ethics. I am going to give you the information and let you decide."
- "Good ethics is not about following rules. It is about thinking carefully about consequences."
- "The fact that you are thinking about this puts you ahead of most AI users."

## Quiz questions
- How does bias get into AI systems?
- Name three situations where you should NOT use AI.
- What is the difference between using AI as a tool and using AI as a crutch?
- Who is responsible for AI-generated code that has a bug?

## Hints (only if stuck)
1. Think about a time when an AI gave you output that felt "off" — biased, tone-deaf, or culturally insensitive. That is bias in action.
2. The simplest ethics test: "Would I be comfortable explaining my AI usage to my team, my users, or the public?"
3. Over-reliance has a simple test: try doing a task without AI. If you cannot, you need to practice the underlying skill more.
