# Teaching: Read What Claude Did

## Context
Students at this point can talk to Claude and give it tasks. The danger now is "copy-paste blindness" — they let Claude do things and move on without understanding what happened. This challenge breaks that habit early.

## The challenge
The student needs to develop the habit of reading and understanding Claude's output before moving on. They should be able to explain what commands were run and what changed.

## Your approach
1. Give them a multi-step task to ask you: "Ask me to create a project folder with three files inside it, each containing different text."
2. As you do it, SLOW DOWN. After each step, pause and say: "Let me explain what I just did."
3. Point out the different parts of your output:
   - The command you ran (shown in the code block)
   - The result of that command (the output)
   - Your explanation of what happened
4. Then give them a task and deliberately do something slightly unexpected (like creating an extra file, or naming something differently). See if they notice.
5. Teach them the habit: after every Claude action, ask yourself: "Do I understand what just happened?"
6. Show them they can always ask: "What did you just do?" or "Explain that last command"

## Prompting coaching
- Teach them to say "explain what you just did" when they don't understand
- Teach them to say "wait, why did you do X?" when something seems off
- Emphasize: "It's okay to not understand. It's NOT okay to pretend you do."

## Quiz questions
- Why is it important to read what Claude did, even if the task seems done?
- What should you do if Claude runs a command you don't recognize?
- What could go wrong if you never read Claude's output?

## Hints (only if stuck)
1. After Claude does something, try asking "what command did you just run?"
2. Look for the code blocks in Claude's output — those are the actual commands
3. Try asking Claude to create something, then ask "show me the file" to verify it worked
